Taste in UX, Below the Surface

Visual polish, whitespace, "clean UI" — you have that. This is the layer underneath: the conceptual model, what to remove, where to place friction, and the opinionated point of view that separates a tool people love from one they merely tolerate.

01The surface is the thin layer

In Jesse James Garrett's model, the visual "surface" is literally the top, thinnest plane of UX. Everything that decides whether a product is good sits below it — strategy, structure, the flows. Taste is overwhelmingly a lower-plane skill.

"The hard part of design is rarely generating the form. It is understanding the problem well enough to know what and how something should exist at all."

Karri Saarinen — founder & CEO, Linear

02It operates on the mental model, not the pixels

The UX equivalent of "pick the right data structure" is design the right conceptual model. Don Norman's frame: the user acts across a gulf of execution ("how do I do this?") and interprets results across a gulf of evaluation ("what just happened?"). The only bridge is the system image — what the interface communicates. Bad UX is almost always a model mismatch, not ugly pixels.

03It's mostly subtraction

The least visible, hardest taste is removal — of choices, steps, screens, and features. Every element added taxes every future user. The greats are unanimous here:

"Good design is as little design as possible. Less, but better — because it concentrates on the essential aspects."

Dieter Rams — Ten Principles of Good Design

Jobs: "deciding what not to do is as important as deciding what to do." Krug: "don't make me think." Golden Krishna pushes it furthest — "the best interface is no interface": sometimes the tasteful answer is to remove the screen entirely and just solve the problem. Subtraction is unglamorous, un-screenshot-able, and the highest-leverage move you have.

04Affordances must be honest

A good interface tells you how to use it without a manual — Norman's affordances (what's possible) and signifiers (what's communicated). Taste here has two halves:

  • Make the right action obvious. The primary path should be the path of least resistance; the dangerous path should take effort.
  • Never lie. The system image must match the system's real state — no fake progress bars, no disabled buttons with no reason, no "Saved!" before it saved. Trust is built by the UI and the system always agreeing.

This is why a chat box alone is often poor taste: a blank text field affords nothing — it hides what the tool can do. Good tools wear their capabilities on their surface.

05Friction is a material, not the enemy

Naive taste says "remove all friction, make it seamless." Mature taste treats friction as a material you place deliberately. Frictionless is wrong when the moment deserves weight:

  • Safety & reversibility — confirmation on destructive actions; undo over "are you sure" where possible.
  • Trust — the "labor illusion": showing the work (a check running, results being gathered) can make an instant result feel more trustworthy, not less.
  • Reflection — a deliberate speed bump before an irreversible or high-stakes commit.

The taste is friction where it matters, none where it doesn't — a gradient, not a global "make everything fast."

06It's opinionated, not consensus

Great UX has a coherent point of view — the experience equivalent of conceptual integrity. It comes from synthesizing the latent need, not averaging stated preferences. Users can rarely name what they want; taste is seeing it before they can.

This is why A/B tests can't produce taste: they climb the nearest local hill. They'll never discover the redesign that requires going downhill first. Linear's whole thesis is craft and conviction over metric-chasing — and it became the most-loved tool in its category by ignoring the consensus playbook.

UX taste, like software taste, is a coherent ranking of values in tension — and great products choose, visibly:

density ⇄ simplicityspeed ⇄ guidancepower ⇄ approachabilityconsistency ⇄ appropriatenessdelight ⇄ restraintflexibility ⇄ opinionnovelty ⇄ familiarity

Blind consistency and reflexive "best practices" are the tell of no taste — knowing when to break a convention (and when muscle memory means you must not) is the skill.

07Taste lives in the edges

Amateurs perfect the happy path — the demo, the hero shot. Taste shows in the states nobody screenshots, because that's where real use actually happens.

Where amateurs stop

  • the happy path
  • the marketing screenshots
  • the hero animation
  • the settings nobody opens

Where taste sweats

  • empty states & the first 5 minutes
  • error messages & failure handling
  • loading, latency & perceived speed
  • sensible defaults (the highest-leverage decision)

Defaults deserve special weight: the option you pre-pick is the one most users will live with forever. A tasteful default is a quiet, enormous act of design.

08It's felt over time, not seen in a frame

UX taste isn't a screenshot; it's the derivative — how the thing feels to use, repeatedly, over weeks. Tempo, responsiveness, the physics of motion, the weight of a tap. That's why the deepest taste refuses to separate design from engineering:

"Most people make the mistake of thinking design is what it looks like… Design is how it works."

Steve Jobs — on Jony Ive's work

Motion is the clearest tell: tasteful motion explains (where did this come from, where did it go — preserving the user's sense of place); tasteless motion decorates. And the oldest name for the felt quality comes from architect Christopher Alexander — the "quality without a name": the hard-to-articulate sense that something is alive, whole, right. You feel it before you can justify it, and chasing that felt rightness is the work.

09Match the aesthetic to the job

There is no universal "good UX," just as there's no single good taste in code. The aesthetic must fit the job to be done (Christensen) and the user:

  • Pro tools want density. Edward Tufte's lesson — maximize information per glance. Bloomberg, vim, Linear, a trading terminal: "clean and minimal" would cripple them. Dense-done-well is high taste.
  • Consumer & first-run want simplicity — fewer choices, gentle slope.
  • Regulated & high-stakes want legibility & honesty over delight — show state, confirm, leave a trail.

The "minimalism everywhere" cargo cult is itself a failure of taste. Reading which aesthetic the situation demands is the taste.

10The dark side

Taste serves the user's goal — the moment it serves a metric or the designer's portfolio, it inverts into its opposite:

  • Engagement-maxxing & dark patterns — friction added to trap (hard-to-cancel, confirm-shaming) is anti-taste wearing a polished coat.
  • Delight as garnish — animation and "joy" piled on a confused flow. Fix the model first; delight is the reward for a solved problem, not a cover for an unsolved one.
  • Beauty over honesty — a gorgeous UI that misrepresents what the system is doing is worse than a plain one that tells the truth.

11The moat — and how it's earned

As AI generates interfaces on demand, the baseline is generic, plausible "UI slop." So judgment — knowing what to build, what feels right, what to cut — becomes the differentiator. This is why a craft renaissance is happening at exactly the moment generation got cheap (Linear, Vercel, Raycast, Arc): taste is the part the model can imitate but can't own, because it's grounded in a point of view.

And it's earned, not downloaded:

  • Exposure — study the greats (Rams, Tufte, Alexander) and live in great products until the bar resets.
  • Dogfood in real context — distracted, mobile, tired, first-time. The demo lies; real use tells the truth.
  • Critique culture — Apple's design reviews, Pixar's "Braintrust": taste is transmitted through honest, frequent critique, not style guides.
  • Own the outcome — feel the consequence of your choices on real users. Taste calibrates on feedback, not opinion.

What you might not have had crisply

  • The visual surface is the thinnest plane — taste decides on strategy & structure (Garrett, Saarinen).
  • UX taste operates on the conceptual model — closing Norman's two gulfs via the system image.
  • Subtraction and defaults are the highest-leverage, least-visible taste.
  • Friction is a material — placed where it matters; "seamless everywhere" is naive.
  • Great UX is opinionated (A/B tests can't find it) and contextual (density vs simplicity by job).
  • It's felt over time (Alexander's quality-without-a-name; Ive's "how it works") — and now the moat, when UI is cheap to generate.
