Taste in Software, Below the Waterline

The surface answer — clean code, good names, nice formatting — you already have. This is the part underneath: what taste actually operates on, why there isn't one "good taste," and what the people who unmistakably have it think it is.

01Drop the surface definition

Formatting, naming, "clean code," picking the trendy framework — that's the visible tip. Taste is mostly the 90% below the waterline: judgment about what's worth building, which representation makes the problem dissolve, and what to leave out. It's compressed, fast, values-driven discrimination — not a style guide.

02Taste is real, not opinion

The first non-obvious claim, and Paul Graham's whole argument in "Taste for Makers": good design is not merely subjective. There are properties that recur across great work — it's simple, it solves the right problem, it looks easy but is hard, it's timeless, and it's often a little daring. Taste is a learned classifier built from thousands of examples, not a vibe.

The practical edge: this lets you separate taste (durable, convergent) from fashion (mimicry — microservices everywhere, the quarterly rewrite, every new framework). Good taste is conspicuously unfashionable: it ages well.

03What it operates on: representation, not style

The deepest code-level taste isn't tidiness — it's choosing the representation that makes complexity disappear. Torvalds' famous example: removing a node from a singly linked list. The naive version needs a special case for the head; the tasteful version makes that special case vanish.

before — a special case for the head

void remove(List *l, Item *t) {
  Item *c = l->head, *p = NULL;
  while (c != t) { p = c; c = c->next; }
  if (p) p->next = c->next;       // middle
  else   l->head = c->next;       // head
}

after — the special case is gone

void remove(List *l, Item *t) {
  Item **p = &l->head;   // pointer-to-pointer
  while (*p != t)
    p = &(*p)->next;
  *p = t->next;          // one path. done.
}

"…rewrite it so that the special case goes away and becomes the normal case. And that's good code."

Linus Torvalds — TED, on good taste

Fred Brooks said the same from the other end: "Show me your flowcharts and conceal your tables, and I shall continue to be mystified. Show me your tables, and I won't usually need your flowcharts." Taste lives in the data model; pick it well and the logic becomes obvious. Most "complex" code is a bad representation wearing cleverness as a disguise.

04There is no single "good taste" — only a coherent ranking of values

Bryan Cantrill's "Software as a Reflection of Values" is the insight most people miss. Every system embodies values — and it cannot maximize all of them. Taste is which ones win when they collide:

simplicityperformancerobustnessapproachabilityvelocityportabilitydebuggabilityexpressivenesssafetystabilityintegritycomposability

SQLite optimizes integrity and stability; Node optimizes approachability and velocity; the Linux kernel optimizes performance and backward-compatibility. All are tasteful — and they'd each call the others' trade-offs wrong. Mature taste isn't a universal scale; it's a prioritized, internally-consistent value system, matched to the job. Brooks' term for applying it uniformly across a whole system is conceptual integrity — and he argued it's "the most important consideration in system design."

"…it's not just about selecting the right tool for the job. It's about selecting the right values for the job — and then the right software for those values."

Bryan Cantrill

05The deepest layer: taste is theory-building

Peter Naur's 1985 "Programming as Theory Building" is the most underread idea in software. His claim: a program is the externalization of a theory the builders hold in their heads — of the domain, and of why the code is shaped the way it is. The source is only a shadow of that theory.

The consequences are the signal. Rot is theory loss, not messy syntax. Code can be immaculately formatted and dead (the team that held the theory left; no one can safely change it), or scruffy and alive (the theory is intact, so changes are easy and correct). Taste, at its deepest, is building and preserving a coherent theory — and recognizing that documentation, tests, and tidy code are aids to it, never replacements for it.

🧠
The reframe

"Maintainable" doesn't mean clean. It means the next person can reconstruct the theory. Tasteful code is legible to the model in someone else's head.

06Mature taste is contextual — dogma is the tell of no taste

The counterintuitive part: the tasteful engineer often does the "uglier" thing on purpose, because rules applied without judgment are the opposite of taste.

  • "Clean code" can be bad taste. Casey Muratori showed the standard clean-code rules (polymorphism over switch, etc.) running ~15× slower. In a hot loop, the tasteful choice is the "dirty" one.
  • Abstraction is a timing decision. Sandi Metz: "duplication is far cheaper than the wrong abstraction." Premature DRY is a classic tell — taste is knowing when to abstract (often: not yet).
  • Simple ≠ easy. Rich Hickey: simple is objective (un-braided, one concern) and often harder; easy is just familiar. Reaching for easy over simple is the most common taste failure.

Cargo-culted patterns, "best practices" recited without their trade-offs, purity for its own sake — these look like taste and are its absence. Real taste is contextual judgment, which is why it can't be a checklist.

07It's felt before it's argued

Ask a top engineer why a design is wrong and you'll often get "I can't articulate it yet, but this is wrong" — followed, a day later, by the precise reason. That's not hand-waving. Taste is a fast, somatic classifier (a System-1 response trained on enormous data); the felt revulsion precedes the explanation. The discipline that separates taste from mere bias is the second step: interrogate the gut, make the reason explicit, and be willing to override it when the context says so.

08The dark side

Taste without restraint is a liability. The most expensive mistakes in our field are tasteful: the elegant rewrite that kills the product (Joel Spolsky's "things you should never do"), the abstraction nobody needed, the year spent polishing what should have shipped rough.

⚠️
Taste serves the outcome, not the ego

Knowing when not to indulge your taste — shipping the good-enough thing, tolerating the ugly seam — is itself the higher taste. Aesthetics in service of the user; never the reverse.

09Why it's suddenly the moat

When generating code was expensive, raw ability was the bottleneck. In 2026 it isn't — agents generate more than anyone can review. So the scarce skill flips from generation to discrimination.

"When execution is no longer scarce, judgment is. The differentiator is no longer the ability to generate ideas, but the ability to discriminate between them."

The 2026 consensus — "code is cheap, judgment is scarce"

The engineer who can glance at AI output and instantly feel what's subtly wrong, what's fragile, what breaks under load, what confuses a user in six months — that's taste, and it's now the differentiator between people and between companies. Taste is the one thing the model can imitate but not own, because it's grounded in your values and your context.

10How the great ones get it

Taste is caught, not taught — there's no rulebook, only these:

  • Exposure to the best. Jobs: taste is "trying to expose yourself to the best things humans have done, and then bring those things into what you're doing." Read great codebases the way writers read great books.
  • Reps with tight feedback. Build, ship, watch it break, feel the consequence. Taste is calibrated by owning outcomes, not by reading about them.
  • Apprenticeship. Code review is the main channel taste travels through — sit near someone who has it.
  • Constraints. Limited memory, time, or scope force the omissions that grow taste. Abundance breeds bloat.

Rick Rubin, from outside our field, says it cleanest: the only thing you ultimately have to offer is your point of view — the work is mostly discernment. That's true of a record and a kernel alike.

What you might not have had crisply

  • Dogma is the tell of no taste. "Clean code," DRY, and purity are contextual — the tasteful move is often the ugly one.
  • Taste is felt first, then interrogated — and its dark side is the beautiful thing that shouldn't have been built.
