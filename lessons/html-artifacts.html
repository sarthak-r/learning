HTML Artifacts & the End of the Static Form

For 20 years, a form was something you built once and sent to everyone. When an LLM can generate a tailored interface on demand, that assumption breaks. Here's what actually changes about collecting information — and why it runs deeper than forms.

01The shift, in one idea

The interface used to be made at build time and shipped identically to everyone. Now it can be made at runtime, shaped for one person and one moment. Collecting information stops being "fill out my form" and becomes "here is an interface generated for you."

🪞
You're looking at one right now

This page is an HTML artifact — self-contained, generated, rendered live in your browser. The thing you're learning about is the medium you're reading it in.

02What an HTML artifact is

An HTML artifact is self-contained, runnable HTML/CSS/JS that a model generates and renders inline — software produced as the output of a conversation. Not prose, not a static document, not a pre-built app. A fourth thing on this ladder:

OutputWhat it isInteractive?Made when?
TextAn answer you readNo—
DocumentA file you keep (PDF, .docx)Barely (fields)Build time
AppSoftware a team shipsYesBuild time
ArtifactBespoke software, generated on demandYesRuntime, per request

The defining trait isn't the HTML — it's when and for whom it's made: disposable, personalized software, conjured for a single context instead of authored once for a market.

03The old world of input-taking

Three tools defined the last era. They differ on the surface but share one DNA: authored once, fixed, identical for all.

ToolStrengthBuilt-in limit
Google FormsFree, instant, structured → spreadsheetRigid, generic, manual skip-logic
TypeformOne-question flow lifts completionStill one script for every respondent
PDF forms (AcroForm)Portable, printable, legal recordFixed layout; clunky to fill & parse

Their shared strengths are exactly what business needs: structured, validated, auditable, predictable data. Their shared weakness is everything that comes from being frozen at authoring time: impersonal, one-size-fits-all, costly to tailor, and longer = more abandonment.

04What changes when the UI is generated

On-demand generation flips four axes at once:

  • Build-time → runtime. The form is created at the moment of need, not before.
  • One-for-all → one-for-one. Tailored to the recipient: pre-filled with what you know, reworded for their context, trimmed to only the relevant questions.
  • Fixed → adaptive. It branches and asks follow-ups based on answers — no rules engine, the model decides.
  • Author fields → describe intent. You state what you need to learn; the artifact is generated. Authoring effort collapses.

05The catch: generated skin, durable spine

Here's the tension that decides everything for business use. A generated interface is non-deterministic and ephemeral — each one is a little different and, by default, remembers nothing. But business data must be consistent, durable, and auditable. Generated structured output is good, not perfect: current models still make sporadic field-level errors, which is why enterprise setups add schema enforcement and trust-scoring.

The resolution is to stop treating the artifact as the thing of record. Split it in two:

🔑
The one principle to keep

Schema-first, presentation-generated. Standardize what must be collected; personalize how it's asked. You get durable, comparable data and a bespoke experience — without choosing between them.

06"Saved as a document" — the 2026 reality

The classic knock on artifacts was that they're throwaway: generated HTML has no backend, so nothing persists. In 2026 that gap is closing fast — persistence and distribution are being built into the platforms:

  • Claude Artifacts now have persistent storage (cross-session, ~20MB each), can be shared to people without an account, remixed, and can call the model's API from inside the artifact. "Live Artifacts" even refresh from connected data sources on reopen.
  • OpenAI Apps SDK + MCP Apps — an open standard (Jan 2026) where a tool returns an interactive UI component that renders inline and works across ChatGPT, Claude, VS Code, and more. Build the experience once; it runs in many AI clients.
  • Generative-UI frameworks (CopilotKit, assistant-ui, Google's A2UI) let you embed runtime-generated components inside your own product, not just a chat app.

So a filled artifact can now behave like a living document — saved, versioned, re-openable, and updatable — rather than a one-shot throwaway. The capability you imagined already partly exists; the open question is where the durable copy lives (the spine).

07Your B2B picture

Your instinct — let your users generate a personalized questionnaire their client fills, saved as an updatable doc — is squarely where this is heading. Conceptually, the lifecycle looks like this:

Where the real value lands:

  • Long-tail forms become economical — every client can get a bespoke questionnaire, not a generic one, at near-zero authoring cost.
  • Personalization lifts completion — relevance and conversational framing measurably reduce drop-off.
  • Your moat isn't the generator — anyone can generate HTML. It's the spine: the schema library, identity, storage, versioning, audit, and workflow your clients trust.
⚖️
The B2B-specific tensions

Trust optics (a one-off generated page collecting sensitive data can look like phishing — branding & provenance matter), accessibility, compliance/audit for regulated data, and consistency across regenerations. These are why the durable spine, not the pretty skin, is the product.

08Pros & cons

✓ Why it's powerful

  • Personalized per recipient & context
  • Adaptive — branches, pre-fills, follows up
  • Near-zero authoring — describe, don't build
  • Higher completion from relevance + conversation
  • Long-tail viable — one-off forms finally pay off
  • Right modality per question (widget vs chat)

✕ Where it bites

  • Non-deterministic — output varies; needs schema lock
  • Field errors — structured output isn't perfect
  • Ephemeral by default — persistence needs a spine
  • Trust/phishing optics for sensitive collection
  • Accessibility & i18n often weak in generated UI
  • Audit/compliance harder when forms vary

09The deeper shift: malleable software

Forms are just the first domino. The real movement underneath is malleable software — the idea that people should reshape their tools at runtime instead of waiting for a vendor to ship an update (Geoffrey Litt & Ink & Switch; the 2025 "Malleable Software" essay, in the lineage of local-first computing).

Why now? End-user programming was always blocked by one wall: turning a rough human idea into formal, working code. LLMs are the missing key that removes it. Once anyone can say what they want and get working software, the unit of software stops being "the app a company ships" and becomes "the interface you generate for this moment."

🧭
The mental flip

What's durable shifts from screens to data + intent. Interfaces become cheap, disposable, and personal; the lasting assets are your structured data and the stated intent that regenerates a view of it on demand.

Seen this way, "generate a client questionnaire" isn't a feature — it's an early instance of software becoming a material you shape rather than a product you receive.

10How to think about it

Not everything should be generated. Choose by what the task values most:

Lean generated / adaptive when…Keep static / schema-locked when…
Discovery, onboarding, intake interviewsRegulated, legal, or high-stakes records
Inputs vary a lot by client/contextEveryone must answer the identical thing
Personalization lifts completionAuditability & exact reproducibility dominate
Long-tail, one-off questionnairesThe form itself is the document of record

And the synthesis: it's rarely either/or. Generate the experience; standardize the data. Let the surface be as personal and adaptive as the moment deserves, while the contract underneath stays boring, fixed, and trustworthy.

Takeaways

  • An HTML artifact is disposable software generated at runtime — the interface moves from build-time to on-demand.
  • Input-taking goes one-for-all → one-for-one: personalized, adaptive, described-not-authored.
  • The decisive principle: schema-first, presentation-generated — generated skin, durable spine.
  • Persistence is being solved (Artifacts storage, MCP Apps, gen-UI frameworks) — but your product owns the system of record, not the generator.
  • Underneath it all: malleable software — durability shifts from screens to data + intent.
