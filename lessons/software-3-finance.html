Software 3.0, Under Supervision

Software 3.0 says: let agents do the work. A regulated industry says: every action must be supervised, recorded, and owned by a human. This is how those two reconcile — using financial advisory (RIAs & wealth) as the test case.

01What "Software 3.0" means here

Karpathy's framing: the LLM is the new compute, English-plus-context is the program, and work happens in a generation → verification loop. The product knob that matters is his autonomy slider — how much the AI does before a human checks. In a regulated industry, you start it low and earn the right to raise it.

So "Software 3.0 for financial advisory" isn't a chatbot bolted onto a CRM. It's agents performing real workflows, wrapped in controls strict enough that a regulator, a client, and your own compliance officer would all sign off.

02The collision

Two value systems meet head-on. Naming the tension is half the design:

Software 3.0 pulls toward…Regulated advisory demands…
Autonomy — agents actSupervision — a named human is accountable (fiduciary duty)
Non-deterministic, emergentDeterministic, reproducible, explainable
Fast natural-language generationRecorded, auditable, retained (books & records)
Ship and iterateValidate & govern before deploy (model risk)
🎯
You don't pick a side

You constrain Software 3.0 with a deterministic governance spine, and keep a licensed human as the fiduciary of record. Autonomy where stakes are low and recorded; humans where it's advice or money.

03The reference architecture

One picture: agents do the work, a deterministic spine governs every action, and a human owns anything that is advice or moves money.

🏰
The reframe that matters

In regulated finance, compliance is the moat, not the tax. The agent layer commoditizes fast; your supervision, records, and trust don't.

05The 2026 regulatory reality

The most important update, because it reverses what most older articles say: regulators chose not to write an AI rulebook. They pointed AI at the rules you already have — and made you own the output.

RegimeWhere it stands (2026)What it means for you
SEC (US)Withdrew the Gensler predictive-analytics conflicts rule (Jun 2025). Atkins: no bespoke AI rules — apply existing frameworks; actively enforces "AI-washing."Your fiduciary, advertising & books-and-records duties already cover AI. Don't overclaim AI in marketing.
FINRATechnology-neutral (Notice 24-09). Supervision (Rule 3110) + recordkeeping apply; 2026 report adds a section on autonomous agents.If a human couldn't do it compliantly, neither can your agent. AI outputs can be business records — capture them.
EU AI ActGPAI/LLM obligations live since Aug 2025; high-risk obligations deferred to Dec 2027 (Digital Omnibus). Credit scoring = high-risk.Transparency & governance now; heavier conformity later if you touch a high-risk use.
NIST (US)AI RMF + AI Agent Standards Initiative (Jan 2026). Guardrails shifting from best-practice → expectation.The de-facto governance checklist examiners and enterprise buyers will reference.
⚠️
Not legal advice — and it moves fast

Rules differ by jurisdiction, license type, and use case, and 2026 is unusually fluid. Treat this as the map, not the territory; confirm specifics with compliance/counsel.

06The business-model shift

  • Service-as-software. Agents deliver outcomes, not features. Pricing drifts from per-seat → usage / credit / outcome-based (Gartner: ≥40% of enterprise SaaS spend usage-, agent-, or outcome-based by 2030).
  • The moat moves down the stack. The UI and the agent layer commoditize (agents call APIs directly). What endures: proprietary client data, operational history, compliance infrastructure, and trust.
  • Selective unbundling. Point AI tools get absorbed; platforms with deep data + compliance depth get stronger. In regulated finance, that depth is the defensibility.
📊
Reality check on timing

73% of advisory firms use AI somewhere, but only ~6% use agentic tools — this is early. Anthropic's Claude for Financial Services (May 2026: ten finance agents, incl. KYC screening & wealth workflows) signals the direction and the pace.

07Build now vs avoid

✓ Build

  • Autonomy slider low by default, raised per low-stakes workflow
  • A deterministic compliance spine you own (not the model vendor's)
  • Hard HITL gates on advice & money movement
  • Record everything — WORM + tamper-evident audit
  • Evals + monitoring (model risk management) before and after deploy
  • Source citations & reasoning trails (explainability)
  • Least-privilege data + PII controls; design for examination

✕ Avoid

  • AI as the adviser of record
  • Full autonomy on advice or trades
  • "AI-washing" — overclaiming AI in marketing
  • Un-archived AI communications
  • Black-box recommendations with no rationale
  • Piping client PII into ungoverned models
  • Bolting compliance on after the demo works

Takeaways

  • Software 3.0 = agents working a generation → verification loop, dialed by an autonomy slider.
  • In regulated advisory, start the slider low; raise it only where stakes are low and the action is recorded.
  • The compliance spine (supervision · WORM/audit · model risk · explainability) is the product — and the moat.
  • 2026 reg reality: no AI rulebook — existing rules apply, you own the output, AI-washing is enforced, EU high-risk deferred to 2027.
  • The human stays the fiduciary of record. AI assists; it never advises.
